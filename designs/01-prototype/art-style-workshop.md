# Volley! Art Style Workshop

A working document for establishing the visual language of Volley! This is not a brief; it is a reference analysis and style exploration that will inform the brief.

---

## The Core Question

Volley! is a game about someone who built a world to hide from grief. The art must support two registers:

1. **The Constructed World**: warm, curated, safe. A small pong arena that someone made to feel okay.
2. **The Real World**: ordinary, lived-in, grounded. A place that feels like someone actually grew up there.

These are not two art styles. They share line work, shapes, and proportions, but they differ in how much comfort they offer. The constructed world is a simplification of the real one: cleaner, softer edges, more saturated colour, more deliberate composition. The real world has more texture, more weight, more life that was not arranged for anyone's comfort. The shift between them should feel like waking up, not like changing the channel.

## References

### Warmth as Construction

These works build warm, inviting spaces that are deliberately constructed by someone, for a reason.

**Porco Rosso** (1992)
Director: Hayao Miyazaki. Art Director: Kazuo Oga. Animation Director: Toshio Kawaguchi.

Miyazaki's sun-drenched Adriatic is the definitive "somewhere someone loved, rendered from memory." Kazuo Oga's watercolour backgrounds are warm without being saccharine; they feel like a real place filtered through nostalgia. The film is also literally about a man hiding behind a different form to avoid dealing with his past. The warmth is real, but it is also a shield.

What to take: the quality of light. Oga's backgrounds feel like late afternoon in a place you used to go. That feeling of warmth that is both genuine and protecting you from something.

**Bo Burnham: Inside** (2021)
Creator: Bo Burnham (writer, director, cinematographer, editor, performer, composer).

One person, one room, elaborate creative lighting to make a confined space feel like an entire world. The room IS the mental state. Burnham built something obsessively detailed inside a box because the alternative was confronting what was outside it.

What to take: making a small space feel like everything. The constructed world lives in a small desktop window, and it should feel like someone has arranged every part of it with care, the way Burnham lit every frame of that room. Also: the emotional arc of building something elaborate to cope.

**La La Land** (2016)
Director: Damien Chazelle. Cinematographer: Linus Sandgren. Production Designer: David Wasco.

The fantasy sequences feel different from reality not because the style changes but because the feeling changes. Sandgren shot on CinemaScope film stock with long sweeping takes; Wasco built a hyper-romanticised Los Angeles. When the film moves to quieter, more grounded scenes, the world cools down. You feel the shift before you notice it.

What to take: colour saturation, lighting, and composition shift together to change how a scene feels. The musical numbers are vivid, saturated, choreographed. The quiet scenes between them are muted, still, natural. Same world, but the colours cool, the light flattens, and the framing loosens. The constructed world should feel like the musical numbers. The real world should feel like the scenes in between.

### Ordinary as Beautiful

These works find visual richness in unglamorous, everyday settings.

**Good Will Hunting** (1997)
Director: Gus Van Sant. Cinematographer: Jean-Yves Escoffier.

Escoffier gave working-class Boston a warm, muted quality with soft natural lighting. Nothing is stylised. The beauty comes from paying attention: the way light comes through a window, the texture of a particular bar, the feeling of a place that has been lived in for a long time. The camera stays close and quiet.

What to take: the real world should feel like this. Not pretty, but honest. The beauty is that someone actually lived here and it shows.

**Odd Taxi** (2021)
Director: Baku Kinoshita. Character Designer: Kazuya Konomoto. Studios: OLM / P.I.C.S.

Deliberately muted, low-key visuals. Characters who feel like real people living ordinary lives, hiding enormous things beneath the surface. Every conversation is loaded. The visual restraint is a conscious choice to let the writing carry. Simple, flat character designs mask a complex mystery.

What to take: visual restraint as narrative strategy. The art should look simple and warm on the surface, the way Odd Taxi looks like a gentle show about a walrus taxi driver until it isn't. It should reward attention.

### Expression from Simplicity

These works prove that simple character designs animated well beat detailed static art.

**Ranking of Kings / Ousama Ranking** (2021)
Director: Yousuke Hatta. Character Designer: Atsuko Nozaki. Studio: WIT Studio.

Childlike, storybook character designs with enormous emotional range. Bojji is a simple shape, but his body language communicates everything. The gap between how simple the characters look and how deeply the show makes you feel is the entire point. WIT Studio's production values elevate the simple designs with rich backgrounds and fluid action.

What to take: this is the production model. Simple designs, expressive through animation, not through detail. The Ranking of Kings approach solves the production efficiency problem while giving enormous emotional range.

**Keep Your Hands Off Eizouken!** (2020)
Director: Masaaki Yuasa. Character Designer: Naoyuki Asano. Studio: Science SARU.

Yuasa's love letter to animation itself. Loose, sketchy, energetic character designs that prioritise movement over model consistency. Science SARU's house style emphasises expressive, fluid, sometimes abstract animation. The joy is in the movement, not the rendering.

What to take: the animation philosophy. Fewer frames, better chosen. Movement as personality. Each animation state should feel like a different emotion, not a different sprite.

**Star Fetchers** (Svavelstickan)
Developer/Artist: LK (Svavelstickan).

Kinetic, anime-influenced animation with fluid character movement. Personality comes from how characters move, not from how much detail they have. A small team (possibly solo) achieving expressive results through animation priority.

What to take: proof that a small production can achieve fluid, characterful animation by focusing effort on movement rather than rendering detail.

### Style as Emotional Register

These works use visual style shifts to communicate narrative shifts.

**To Be Hero X** (2025)
Director: Li Haoling. Studios: BeDream / PB Animation / Studio LAN / Paper Plane.

Brilliant use of different visual styles and media within a single work. The style shifts are not gimmicks; they are emotional punctuation. When the visual register changes, you feel it before you understand it.

What to take: To Be Hero X proves you can shift palette, line weight, and rendering approach within a single work without losing coherence, as long as the emotional core holds.

**Everything Everywhere All at Once** (2022)
Directors: Daniel Kwan, Daniel Scheinert (the Daniels). Cinematographer: Larkin Seiple. Production Designer: Jason Kisvarday.

Holds coherence across wildly different visual modes because the emotional core is consistent. Seiple used varied lighting and colour grading to visually distinguish each universe. Kisvarday built mundane-to-fantastical environments on a limited budget using creative practical design.

What to take: coherence through emotional consistency. Different visual registers can coexist as long as they feel like they belong to the same person. The emotional throughline is what holds it together.

### Deep World from Specific Details

These works build environments where every element is considered and rewards attention.

**Rain World** (Videocult)
Artist/Designer/Programmer: Joar Jakobsson.

One person created the procedural animation system, the ecosystem-driven creature designs, and the painterly environmental art by hand. Every element in the world feels interconnected. The environment tells its own story without dialogue.

What to take: environmental storytelling through detail. Nothing is generic. Everything was placed by someone who was thinking about something. Rain World proves a single artist can build a world that feels vast through care.

**Oddworld: Abe's Oddysee / Exodus** (1997/1998)
Director: Lorne Lanning. Production Designers: Farzad Varahramyan, Steven Olds.

A world that rewards looking closely. Varahramyan and Lanning co-created the "Oddworld Signature": six artistic commandments that governed every piece of art produced for the games. The world is consistent because it was governed by principles, not just taste.

Their six commandments state that all images must:

1. **Be realistic**: ground the fantastical in believable visual principles
2. **Have solidity**: possess weight and structural integrity; environments feel substantial
3. **Be original**: avoid derivative or recycled concepts; display distinctive vision
4. **Be cool**: maintain visual appeal and style
5. **Evoke emotion**: connect players to the atmosphere and narrative; generate feeling
6. **Look alive**: convey vitality and a sense of living presence

What to take: art direction needs principles, not just references. Commandments give an artist a framework for making decisions independently.

### Readable at Small Scale

These works pack personality into limited visual real estate.

**Enter the Gungeon** (Dodge Roll Games)
Artist: Joe Harty.

Chunky, readable sprite work that makes bullet-hell chaos legible. Every enemy, gun, and room communicates at a glance. Personality packed into small frames through silhouette and colour, not detail.

What to take: everything must read at small scale. Silhouette and colour over rendering. If you squint and it is still clear, it works.

**Saltsea Chronicles** (Die Gute Fabrik)
Art Director: Nils Deneken.

Risograph-print-inspired, storybook illustration style. Solid colours doing enormous work. No complex rendering; the palette and composition carry everything. Clean, readable, warm.

What to take: solid colour as a tool, not a limitation. Confident colour choices read as intentional, not under-produced. Saltsea proves you can carry complex emotion with a restrained rendering approach.

**Poco** (Whalefall)
2D/3D hybrid with hand-painted backgrounds and cartoony 3D animation. Vibrant, varied colour palette applied to cluttered environments filled with forgotten objects.

What to take: vibrant does not mean noisy. Poco's palette is wide but controlled. Each environment has its own colour identity within the broader range. Each object can have its own feeling within the overall world.

---

### Retro Feel and Rhythm

**Baby Driver** (2017)
Director: Edgar Wright. Cinematographer: Bill Pope. Production Designer: Marcus Rowland.

Meticulously choreographed action-to-music editing. Every cut, every movement, every colour choice serves the rhythm. Pope's kinetic camerawork tracks musical choreography. Rowland designed stylised, colour-coded settings.

What to take: rhythm as visual principle. A game loop has a rhythm. The visual language should support that: reactions that feel like beats, breaks that feel like rests. Visual rhythm is felt before it is noticed.

## The Oddworld Precedent

Oddworld's "six artistic commandments" gave Varahramyan's team a framework for producing over 1,000 unique screens with visual consistency. The commandments were not style rules; they were quality principles that every piece of art had to satisfy regardless of content.

Their approach: define the non-negotiable qualities of every image, then trust artists to find their way there. This is the difference between a style guide (which shows what things look like) and a set of commandments (which define what things must feel like).

---

## The Volley! Commandments

Four principles that govern every piece of art in the game. These are not style rules; they are qualities. Any asset, any screen, any register.

### Intentional

Every mark, colour, and placement is a choice. Nothing is filler, nothing is accidental, nothing is "just a background." If it is in the frame, someone decided it should be there.

This is what connects Burnham's obsessively lit room, Oga's watercolour afternoons, and Rain World's hand-placed ecosystems. The feeling that every element was considered.

### Specific

This object, this space, this person. Never generic. Every element should feel like it belongs to this world and no other.

This is what connects Good Will Hunting's sense of place, Odd Taxi's ordinary lives, and Oddworld's six commandments demanding originality. The feeling that you could not swap any element out without it being wrong.

### Distinct

Communicates through shape and colour. Unmistakable at any size. If two things could be confused at a glance, one of them needs to change.

This is what connects Enter the Gungeon's readability, Saltsea's confident solid colours, and Ranking of Kings' simple silhouettes carrying enormous emotion. The feeling that everything announces itself clearly.

### Breathing

Feels alive. Movement carries personality. Still frames should feel like they are about to move.

This is what connects Eizouken's animation philosophy, Star Fetchers' fluid characters, Ranking of Kings' emotional body language, and Poco's vibrant clutter. The feeling that the world is not waiting for the player; it is already going.

## What This Means for the Style

The references point toward: **hand-drawn illustration with bold shapes, limited but confident colour, and expression through animation rather than detail.**

Not pixel art. Not painterly realism. Not vector-clean. Somewhere in the territory of Ranking of Kings' character simplicity, Saltsea's colour confidence, Porco Rosso's warmth, and Eizouken's movement philosophy. A style where a small team can produce expressive results by putting effort into animation and colour rather than rendering complexity.

The constructed world uses this style with saturated colour, clean edges, soft lighting, and considered composition: every element placed with care. The real world shares the same shapes and proportions but loosens the lines, desaturates the palette, lets the light fall where it falls, and leaves things where they actually are rather than where someone arranged them.

The Break is the moment the player sees both registers and understands that the first was always a simplification of the second.
